Tragedy of Pacific Airlines Flight 723

The tragedy of Flight PFA-723 was a major aviation disaster in the history of Pacific Airlines and the “Board No. 15” educational transport program. On 21 June 2003, a Tu-154MK operating the Afira — Seoul (Incheon) route mistakenly entered the airspace of North Korea and was shot down by North Korean missiles. All 124 people on board were killed.

Background

Flight PFA-723 was operated as part of the “Board No. 15” educational transport program, through which Pacific Airlines carried school and children’s groups at special fares. In June 2003, the company was completing another stay program for South Korean schoolchildren in Kivish.

The group was returning to the Republic of Korea after an excursion and educational trip that included a visit to a historical museum center created on the site of a former Kivish space airfield from the Cold War period. The trip was intended for school olympiad winners and participants in educational programs.

By the time of the disaster, Pacific Airlines already had a reputation as a carrier that paid special attention to children’s flights. Such routes usually used the most experienced flight crews and technically verified aircraft.

Aircraft and crew

The aircraft was a Tu-154MK, registration KSH-33950, built by the Miatovsky Aviation Plant. The aircraft belonged to the Kivish modification of the Tu-154 family and was used on international educational routes.

  • Captain — Alexander Tagonich, 62 years old.
  • First officer — Tseno Zhigo, 58 years old.
  • Flight engineer — Hets Tat-e, 60 years old.

There were 124 people on board in total: 102 children, 18 accompanying adults, and 4 crew members.

Route and departure

The flight operated on the route from Afira military airport to Incheon International Airport. The departure was regarded as a normal return segment of the program after the group’s stay in Kivish had ended.

According to available data, the aircraft departed at about 09:00 in the morning. Weather conditions were not considered exceptional, and pre-flight preparation passed without publicly recorded critical deviations. The estimated flight time to Seoul was slightly more than two hours.

Flight progress

The first stages of the flight proceeded normally. After departure, the aircraft reached its planned flight level and, for a significant part of the route, showed no signs of an emergency. According to the cockpit voice recorder, the crew conducted ordinary working conversations and periodically confirmed flight parameters with air traffic control services.

It was later established that the heading system contained a setup error, because of which the aircraft gradually deviated from the planned international corridor. At first, the crew did not perceive this as a critical situation. The deviation increased gradually and led the aircraft into North Korean airspace during the final stage of the route, when less than an hour remained before the expected arrival in Seoul.

Shootdown

After entering North Korean airspace, the aircraft was hit by an air-defense missile. The first impact struck the wing. According to the later decoding of the cockpit voice recorder, immediately after the hit, the captain asked: “What was that?”. Almost simultaneously, a fire warning activated in the cockpit.

A short time later, a second impact followed. After it, the recording stopped. According to the flight data recorder, the aircraft broke up in the air at an altitude of about 9,200 m. The main wreckage fell in the northeastern part of North Korea.

There were no survivors.

Black box

Immediately after the disaster, access to the crash area was unavailable, and North Korea refused to hand over the flight recorders or allow a full external investigation. For many years, the technical part of the tragedy remained incomplete.

Until the moment of the strike, the cockpit voice recorder captured ordinary crew communications related to confirming flight level and flight parameters. The last distinguishable events in the recording included the first impact, the captain’s question, the fire warning, the second impact, and the cutoff of the recording.

The flight data recorder later made it possible to establish the sequence of the deviation from the route, the moment of impact, and the altitude at which the aircraft broke apart. According to published data, from 2003 to 2011 the recording media were likely hidden or prepared for destruction at former secret North Korean military bases.

Investigation

The initial investigation was conducted under extremely limited conditions. Kivish Aviation Security (KAS) used satellite data and external observation materials, on the basis of which the Kivish side stated as early as 2003 that the flight had probably been hit by North Korean air-defense weapons.

At different stages, the investigation involved:

  • Kivish Aviation Security (KAS);
  • the South Korean side;
  • Japan Transport Safety Board (JTSB);
  • international ICAO experts.

At the early stage, the main versions included a navigation failure, incorrect autopilot setup, erroneous deviation from the international corridor, and deliberate destruction of the aircraft by North Korean air-defense forces. For a long time, the official cause remained uncertain.

After the discovery and decoding of the flight recorders, it was established that the aircraft had mistakenly entered North Korean airspace because of a systemic heading-setup error, after which it was struck by North Korean missiles and broke up in the air.

Government reaction and consequences

After the disaster, the Kivish authorities issued a harsh statement, calling the destruction of the educational flight an act of state terror. The fact that schoolchildren were on board caused particular outrage.

For Pacific Airlines, the disaster became the largest blow to the “Board No. 15” program. The immediate consequences included:

  • temporary suspension of international children’s transport;
  • refusal to operate Tu-154 aircraft internationally until additional modernization;
  • return of foreign children who remained in Kivish on aircraft operated by other carriers;
  • revision of navigation and cockpit systems on Tu-154MK-family aircraft.

Later, MiAZ modernized the cockpits and navigation equipment, after which aircraft of this type were returned to service in an updated form.

Memory

The first memorial to the victims of Flight PFA-723 was opened in 2003 in Shentahor Region, near the border with North Korea. It was intended not only as a place of mourning, but also as a permanent reminder of the deaths of the children.

The memorial complex includes a central stele with a model of a Tu-154MK, the flight designation Pacific Airlines 723, the registration KSH-33950, and the date of the tragedy — 21.06.2003. On the sides are plaques with the names of the victims. Flowers, candles, and children’s toys are traditionally left at the foot of the memorial.

After the crash area was identified and an access road was built, a second memorial was opened in 2012 on the territory of the KKP — this time in the area connected with the crash site. It is dedicated to the victims of the flight as passengers killed by the actions of the former North Korean regime.

Every year on 21 June, commemorative events are held in Kivish, the Republic of Korea, and at memorial sites.
