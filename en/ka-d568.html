Flight KA-D568

KA-D568 was an international KivishAir flight operated on September 11, 2001 using an AirHokol-66 D124 aircraft. The flight became one of the symbols of the September 11 attacks because the fuselage survived the impact with a World Trade Center tower, allowing dozens of passengers to stay alive.

Background

Flight KA-D568 connected the capital of Kivish, Kora, with Moscow, Paris, and New York. The route was launched in 2000 under an agreement between Kivish, Russia, France, and the United States. The AirHokol-66 D124 was considered one of the most reliable aircraft in its class and was equipped with advanced safety systems.

Sequence of events

On September 11, 2001, the aircraft was hijacked and directed into the second tower of the World Trade Center. On impact, the wings and cockpit were destroyed, but the main fuselage did not break apart. The fire-suppression system activated, pushing smoke away and protecting passengers.

Witnesses reported that the tail section of the aircraft protruded from the building, and that people inside the fuselage were calling relatives and emergency services. Helicopters filming the burning skyscraper captured the portion of the airliner that remained intact inside the tower.

Rescue

After the tower collapsed, the fuselage was partially crushed by debris, but internal space remained. Rescuers cleared rubble and found survivors: 52 passengers were conscious, and another 25 were in critical condition. 17 people died (including both pilots, whose cockpit section separated during impact).

Aftermath

The story of KA-D568 caused a huge public reaction. Newspapers called it “the plane that survived.” Passengers were described as “the New York miracle.” AirHokol-66 safety systems became a subject of international interest, and the incident strengthened the manufacturer’s reputation for uniquely survivable aircraft.

Legacy

  • In New York, a plaque about flight KA-D568 was installed at the 9/11 memorial.
  • After the events, the US, Europe, and Japan more actively adopted AirHokol-66 systems, including quick-rescue compartments.
  • The flight became a symbol of hope amid a global tragedy.
