D-001

F-KAP D-001 was the first civilian car produced by F-KAP, released in 1921. The model became the starting point of the early D-Series line and the plant’s first production experience in creating a vehicle designed for operation in a harsh climate.

Background

The F-KAP plant was built on the outskirts of Kora, in the working district of Retnikov, where workshops and small enterprises were located at the time. Against the background of post-war devastation and a shortage of reliable transport for officials, doctors, and merchants, the idea emerged to create a domestic car suitable for daily use and winter conditions.

Development

The D-001 project was developed as a utilitarian vehicle. The main priority was practicality, not appearance or equipment. The design was shaped around the tasks of winter operation, simple maintenance, and minimal dependence on complicated repairs. The model was viewed as working transport that had to start and drive through snow and frost with only basic care.

Technical concept

The D-001 was equipped with a 19-horsepower engine. For driving on winter roads, winter tires with metal elements in the tread were used. The key feature was the engine preheating system using a wood-fired stove. This solution became a signature feature of the early D-line and was used as a practical way to reduce the risk of failure when starting in severe cold.

Layout and operating solutions

Early cars of the series, including the D-001, were built as simple two-seat bodies with a folding canvas top. The design included a rear box for firewood, allowing a reserve of wood to be carried for warming the engine in winter and avoiding dependence on random fuel sources. The approach was focused on everyday-use scenarios, where the car could stand in freezing temperatures and require warming before driving.

Delivery package

The early delivery packages emphasized the company’s service philosophy. F-KAP positioned itself as a driver-friendly manufacturer and supplied the D-001 with extra firewood, winter tools, and instructions for lighting the stove. Contemporaries noted that the car was perceived more as working equipment than as a luxury item.

Distribution

The D-001 and the first cars of the D-line quickly spread through government garages and rural areas. The practicality of the design, adapted for winter, made the model popular in places where ordinary transport often proved unpredictable in frost and snow.

Reception

Despite its simplicity, the D-001 had a noticeable effect on the local market. Company sources emphasize that residents received, for the first time, the ability to drive through snow and frost without the typical risk of stopping because of a freezing engine. This effect was associated with the combination of winter tires and the engine heating system, as well as with the fact that the manufacturer immediately supplied the car with a winter operating kit.

Legacy

The D-001 established the early technical image of F-KAP as a manufacturer that built cars around operating conditions and the habits of the driver. The experience of the first model became the basis for the further evolution of the D-line and established wood-fired engine preheating as one of the company’s recognizable early solutions. Within the brand’s historiography, the D-001 is often regarded as the starting point of F-KAP’s entire civilian school.

D-002

F-KAP D-002 was a modernized version of the plant’s first model, released in 1922. The car was created as a direct response to problems revealed during the first operation of the D-001 and became one of the early examples in the series where changes were dictated by everyday conditions and safety.

Background

The first owners of the D-001 encountered a set of typical winter difficulties. In practice, three problems appeared most quickly: insufficient heat retention inside the cabin, fogging of the glass, and poor visibility of the car from behind in a snowstorm. For the driver, this meant worse visibility and fatigue from the cold; for surrounding traffic, it meant an increased risk of collisions in snow and poor visibility.

Development

When creating the D-002, the plant did not aim to completely change the basic design. Unlike the approach of “a new model instead of the old one,” the D-002 was formed as a correction of the D-001 while preserving overall simplicity. The project was based on the logic of targeted improvements: closing the cabin, sealing it, ensuring visibility control, and making the car more visible on the road.

Cabin and heat retention

The main external change was the solid metal closed cabin. It reduced drafts and made the temperature inside more stable. In addition, F-KAP used the first seal, designed to keep heat inside the cabin and reduce direct losses through gaps and joints. As a result, the car became more suitable for long winter trips and standing in the cold.

Anti-fogging solution

Fogging of the glass in frost and during temperature changes was treated as a separate operating problem. In the D-002, a simple and visible method was used: an internal wiper, which duplicated the work of the outside wipers and allowed the glass surface to be cleaned from inside the cabin. The solution was crude in execution, but practical in result, since it directly affected visibility and safety.

Visibility in a snowstorm

A separate task was to improve the readability of the car from behind. In heavy snowfall, the rear of the car often “disappeared,” creating situations where following traffic recognized the obstacle too late. To reduce this risk, the D-002 received one rear red lamp, which worked as a beacon and helped identify the presence of a car ahead more quickly.

Operation

The D-002 preserved the general philosophy of early F-KAP cars as utilitarian transport. The model remained simple to maintain, but became noticeably more convenient in winter conditions. The insulated closed cabin and the visibility solution reduced driver fatigue, while the rear lamp reduced the number of dangerous situations in snowstorms.

Reception and significance

In the early history of the plant, the D-002 is considered the first car where F-KAP demonstrated the principle of serial refinement based on real owner problems. The D-002’s solutions established directions that would later be developed further: climate adaptation, anti-fogging measures, road readability, and practical safety measures. These themes later became the basis for the D-010 and D-020, where the series moved from targeted fixes to a more complex logic of all-season operation and developed lighting equipment.

D-010

F-KAP D-010 was a 1925 model that became an important stage in the development of the early D-line. When creating it, the plant attempted to solve a problem that appeared on the D-002: the car performed well in winter, but in warm weather the cabin could overheat. The D-010 was viewed as a more universal car, suitable for operation in different seasons and climates.

Background

After the release of the D-002, the plant encountered the reverse side of the closed insulated cabin. Practice showed that in warm weather the car could become too hot both in Kora and in other cities. The task of the next version was to preserve winter suitability while adding controlled ventilation and a summer operating mode.

Development and seasonal body layout

In the D-010, the approach of the early design was brought back, but reworked functionally. The model received a folding roof, described in brand sources as the line’s first early analogue of a convertible layout. At the same time, a system of lowering side windows on the doors was introduced, as well as an opening windshield designed for airflow mode. As a result, the driver could quickly change the character of the cabin depending on the weather without abandoning the basic winter logic.

Testing in different climates

The D-010 was tested in summer and winter conditions in several cities. Test cycles were conducted in winter and summer in Kora, in winter and summer in Beijing, and in winter and summer in Moscow. Based on the results, engineers rejected some solutions, keeping only what was considered necessary while preserving the base, chassis, and key elements of seasonal adaptation.

Summer mode: ventilation and windshield

In summer mode, the driver could lower the windshield using a handle, opening airflow and reducing cabin overheating. In rainy weather, the reverse scenario was provided: the windshield was raised, while the side window could be lowered partially, roughly halfway, which was considered the maximum opening in that mode. This allowed controlled ventilation without direct entry of precipitation.

Heating: revised heater activation layout

As part of the D-010, the logic of turning on the heater was changed. The previously used layout was perceived ambiguously by users, so lever control was introduced instead of a tap. Under the hood, a valve was installed and linked to the lever and the cooling system. When the valve was opened, the fluid flow was directed through the heater installed in the cabin, and a small heater under the driver’s feet began warming the cabin. After passing through the heater, the fluid returned under the hood and was then cooled through the radiator.

This solution made it possible to regulate the heat level: in the open position, the cabin warmed up; in the closed position, heating decreased, reducing the risk of overheating inside the cabin. In cases where it became too hot, it was possible to switch to a more open body mode and use the folding roof and ventilation.

Lighting equipment: clarification of rear signals

After the introduction of the rear red lamp on the D-002, drivers became more careful in snowstorms, but a new problem appeared: a single lamp did not show when the car was braking, and the driver behind might not react in time, especially in fog and snowfall. On the D-010, the rear light signaling was made more complex: instead of one lamp, two were used. One acted as a constant rear light, while the second lit up when the brake was pressed, separating the fact of the car’s presence from the fact of slowing down.

Model designations and name unification

The D-010 period is also associated with a change in the approach to naming. The company noted that transitions between different language standards caused confusion, so later model designations began to be formed according to a simpler and more unified principle, with a focus on English-language words used in British English to make the perception and classification of products easier.

Significance

The D-010 became the model where the early F-KAP line first received a systematic seasonal logic: not only winter survival, but also a controlled summer mode. At the same time, the D-010 established the direction of light-signaling development by separating the rear light from the brake light, which would later become the basis for more complex solutions on the D-020.

D-020

F-KAP D-020 was a 1927 model that became one of the key stages of the pre-war D-line. When creating it, F-KAP formally organized driver feedback from Kora for the first time and used it as the basis for a list of changes. As a result, the D-020 became noticeably more mature compared with the D-010 and established the series’ transition from utilitarian logic toward urban and family use.

Background

By the mid-1920s, the D-line had already received basic seasonal body and heating adaptation. At the same time, questions remained regarding passenger accommodation, the readability of the car in city traffic, and the general “civilized” character of the cabin. Lighting equipment remained a separate problem: early solutions improved visibility, but still did not provide a full signal language for other drivers. The D-020 was conceived as a response to these tasks.

Collecting feedback

Brand sources emphasize that before launching the D-020, engineers began asking drivers in Kora direct questions about what changes were needed in the next version. This approach differed from the early practice, when improvements were introduced mostly on the designers’ own initiative. For the D-020, the changes were recorded as specific requirements related to seating, comfort, signals, and urban operation.

Body and cabin layout

The D-020 received a five-seat layout: two separate front seats and a rear bench. This was an important step, since the model began to focus not only on working use, but also on family trips and urban everyday life. The body was again made fully closed, but elements considered useful for ventilation and seasonal flexibility were preserved: a folding windshield and opening side windows.

Inside the cabin, more comfortable trim appeared, along with a cutout for a small portable radio device. For the late 1920s, this was regarded as a sign of transition toward a more “personal” car, where the cabin was designed not only as the driver’s place, but also as a space for passengers.

Heating and heater placement

One of the characteristic solutions of the D-020 was the changed placement of the heater. The heating unit was moved into the engine bay so that loading firewood and maintenance could be performed under the hood. This approach preserved the general philosophy of the early line, where preheating and heating were considered mandatory parts of winter operation, while also improving service convenience and reducing interference with the cabin layout.

Lighting equipment and Figir’s patent

The most noticeable innovation was the developed light-signaling system. Engineer Figir patented a full rear lamp in an iron housing. The unit was made as a square element with four sections: running light, brake light, orange turn signal, and flashing reverse signal. This division of functions made the car’s behavior clearer to surrounding traffic in fog and snowfall and reduced the risk of maneuvers being misread.

At the front, a small orange turn repeater appeared next to the main headlight. Inside the cabin, on the panel next to the steering wheel, a switch was installed: when moved left, the left signal flashed; when moved right, the right one flashed. For the series, this meant a transition from simple “beacons” to a full system of communication between the car and traffic flow.

Passive safety

Series sources note that early analogues of seat belts were used on the D-020 in the form of black canvas straps. These elements were intended to hold a person during a collision and were considered an experimental safety measure that would later become one of the directions in the development of civilian models.

Aerodynamics and efficiency

The D-020 received a redesigned body shape intended to reduce air resistance. In descriptions of the model, this was linked to improved fuel efficiency and more stable behavior at speed. Compared with earlier versions, the series began to take into account not only starting and driving through snow, but also energy efficiency as a characteristic of everyday operation.

Engine and dynamics

Engine power was increased to 43 horsepower. For the D-line, this meant a significant increase compared with the earliest cars and made the D-020 more confident in urban driving and when carrying passengers. The increase in power was accompanied by a general attempt to make the car more mature and convenient, not simply “faster.”

Significance

The D-020 is regarded as the model on which the D-line took shape as a family of civilian cars intended for urban and family use. It established key directions for further development: separate lighting equipment with turn signals, a more convenient cabin layout, early attempts at passive safety, and work with body shape for the sake of efficiency. These solutions became the basis for the following pre-war leaps of the series in the 1930s.

D-030 and D-031

F-KAP D-030 and F-KAP D-031 were 1930 models that are considered one of the main pre-war turning points in the development of the D-line. At this stage, F-KAP focused not only on operating improvements, but also on appearance, turning the series into a visually notable mass-market car. Against the background of cars from other manufacturers, the D-030 and D-031 stood out sharply in body shape and were perceived as one of the first examples of a “beautiful” civilian car of the Soviet period of the UKR.

Background

By the end of the 1920s, the D-line had already received a mature lighting system, a family layout, and a set of solutions for winter operation. However, the appearance of the early cars remained utilitarian and functional. At the same time, cities were developing demand for a more civilized and neat-looking car that would preserve affordability and practicality but would not look like “workshop equipment.” The D-030 and D-031 were launched as a response to this demand.

Design and aerodynamics

The main feature of the D-030/031 was the deliberate simplification and smoothing of the shape. The body was made as smooth as possible: the number of protruding elements was reduced, excessive corners and sharp transitions were removed, and the body lines were shaped so that the car would be perceived as a single volume. Model descriptions emphasized that these changes were made not only for aesthetics, but also for aerodynamics, which was associated with fuel efficiency and more stable movement.

Paintwork was also important to the visual image: for the first time, the model received more attractive color options aimed at the mass buyer. Against the background of “angular” cars from other companies, this made the D-030/031 recognizable at first glance.

Equipment levels and version differences

The D-030 and D-031 established the line’s principle of dividing equipment by climate and price. Series sources emphasize the existence of two main approaches to equipment.

  • High northern equipment level included solutions for cold weather and more comfortable contact with cabin materials: a heater as part of the brand’s winter philosophy, fabric covering on metal elements to avoid touching cold metal, rubberized handles, a small built-in cabin fan, and a reinforced belt design. This version also used a built-in radio.
  • Cheap warm-climate equipment level excluded a number of expensive elements: the heater, radio, and some “comfort” solutions intended for winter conditions were not installed. This version was intended for regions and operating scenarios where winter equipment was considered excessive.

Chassis and wheels

The models received larger-diameter wheels. This was associated with the car’s practical suitability for roads of varying quality and with the general “maturity” of the model, which now had to be a universal mass-market car rather than a narrowly specialized vehicle of the early period.

Positioning and accessibility

In the pre-war historiography of the brand, the D-030/031 are often described as the moment when a “beautiful car” became accessible to a wide range of buyers. Within the series, these models are regarded as the transition from the utilitarian logic of the early D models to a full mass-market civilian car intended for an ordinary owner, not only for service garages.

Significance

The D-030 and D-031 established F-KAP’s reputation as a manufacturer capable of making not only practical and winter-resistant equipment, but also a modern-looking mass-market car. The principle of dividing equipment by climate and price became the basis for subsequent models of the early 1930s, while the design line of these cars prepared the ground for the further evolution of the series’ pre-war appearance.

D-033 and D-034

F-KAP D-033 and F-KAP D-034 were 1931 models that appeared as a response to practical comments from owners about the equipment of the D-030/031. In these versions, F-KAP reworked the concept of “built-in” devices and emphasized modularity, the possibility of later equipment installation, and a more flexible equipment system.

Background

The northern rich equipment version of the D-030/031 used a built-in radio. However, in Kora this solution caused everyday criticism: owners noted that the radio was tied to the car and could not be taken with them after stopping. The situation was made worse by a practical issue: listening to the news near home required keeping the car on, which was perceived as unnecessary fuel consumption and inconvenience. These comments were recorded as a direct request for equipment revision.

Development and the principle of modularity

In the D-033 and D-034, F-KAP changed its overall approach to equipment. Instead of making expensive equipment an inseparable part of the car, the plant introduced modular solutions that allowed the device either to be removed and used separately or purchased later. Thus, the owner could choose equipment according to actual need, not only according to the factory trim level.

Removable F-KAP radio

The key innovation was the removable radio. A mounting cutout was provided for it in the dashboard, and connection was made through special contacts designed to operate from the car’s electrical system. After stopping, the radio unit could be removed and turned into a portable F-KAP radio.

For home use, variants of a “station” were offered, onto which the radio unit could be installed. Depending on the version, this could be a simple stand powered from a wall socket or a separate home center with speakers that provided better sound. This approach expanded the equipment’s usage scenarios: the radio ceased to be only a car function and became a household device.

Removable heater and detachable connections

In the senior D-033 equipment version, engineers used an even more radical idea. The heater was made removable by replacing welded connections with detachable ones using threaded pipe fittings. The design provided for shutting off the valves, after which the heating module could be removed and the included bypass pipe installed in its place, allowing the car to continue operating without the heater.

The removed heating module was intended to be used as an autonomous heat source for warming a small room, such as a room or small apartment. Within the historiography of the line, this solution is regarded as a characteristic example of F-KAP’s focus on the owner’s everyday scenarios, not only on the movement of the car.

Base version and upgrade logic

In the cheap D-034 equipment version, the heater and radio were absent by default. Instead of the heater, a pipe was installed, and the place for the radio was closed with a plastic blanking plate. At the same time, the plant introduced the official possibility of purchasing equipment separately. The owner could buy a radio with the required station version, as well as a heater for winter operation if it was needed. In this way, equipment became gradual and more accessible.

Additional options

As a separate mini-option, a lamp for illuminating the door opening area was offered. It was not included in any equipment level by default, but could be installed at the owner’s request. This approach complemented the general idea of the D-033/034 as a family where important small conveniences were moved into the category of official factory improvements.

Reception and significance

The D-033 and D-034 are regarded as the stage where F-KAP established the user-oriented character of the pre-war line. The removable radio became a response to direct owner criticism, while the concept of a detachable heater and factory upgrade options formed the image of the car as a system that could be adapted to everyday life. Within the D-Series, these models are usually described as a transition from “ready-made equipment” to a modular approach that influenced the further development of the brand’s civilian cars.

D-038

F-KAP D-038 was the final civilian model of the pre-war D-line, introduced in 1938. By that time, the basic versions of the series, including the D-030/031 and D-033/034, continued to sell almost unchanged because they remained modern and in demand. However, the old body shapes, especially the characteristic arches and transitions from the arch to the main body volume, no longer felt like a noticeable breakthrough and began to look familiar. In response, F-KAP made a new design leap without radically changing the car’s dimensions.

Background

Sources on the line emphasize that in the 1930s the series became so successful that it could “live for decades” without regular updates. Nevertheless, by the end of the decade, the appearance of the early pre-war generation had become established and familiar. Since competitors were gradually catching up in overall design level, the plant decided to update the model in order to preserve visual modernity and improve aerodynamic qualities.

Body redesign

The D-038 received a reworked exterior while preserving the general platform logic. The purpose of the update was to make the body neater and more unified, reduce the visual “stepped” character of the lines, and improve the perception of the shape among new cars. The key direction was the reworking of the areas around the wheel arches and the transitions between the arches and the main body volume. Instead of the previous pronounced “arch → transition → body” layout, smoother joints were used, making the car look more attractive and more mature.

Aerodynamics and visual proportion

Model descriptions separately noted the desire to improve aerodynamics. At the same time, the plant tried to achieve the effect of the car being “wider” and more confident on the road without actually increasing its dimensions. In meaning, this was a design leap aimed at making the appearance more modern while preserving the efficiency achieved in the 1930s.

Place in the line

The D-038 is considered the final stage of the civilian evolution of the D-Series. The model completed the pre-war phase of the series, gathering the key ideas of previous years: practicality, climate suitability, mass-market character, and orientation toward real operation, but in a neater and more modern body design.

End of the civilian line

After the release of the D-038, further development of the civilian D-line was limited by growing international tension. Against the background of reports of an approaching major war, Akolon instructed F-KAP to transition toward the creation of military equipment. From 1940, the enterprise was involved in helping China in the war against Japan. As a result, the pre-war civilian D-Series line effectively ended, and the plant was reoriented toward defense tasks.
