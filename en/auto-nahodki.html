Auto-Nahodki

Auto-Nahodki is a Kivish system for handling abandoned and found vehicles, as well as the largest national listings platform for buying and selling cars. The project emerged during the era of the UKR as a newspaper page in 1954, and then went through a consistent digital evolution: from a text-based network catalogue in 1992 to a web platform that fully replaced print in 2010.

Historically, Auto-Nahodki solved two tasks: clearing streets of abandoned transport and creating a civilized secondary market with affordable used cars for the population. In its modern form, the platform combines two flows: the sale of found vehicles after state investigations and private listings from users and companies.

Background

In the 1950s, the number of cars in the cities of the UKR increased sharply. At the same time, the first mass cases of abandoned transport appeared: cars were left in courtyards and on streets, parking areas became clogged, and some owners left the country while leaving property behind without proper paperwork. Destroying the cars was unprofitable: a significant part of the fleet remained relatively new and suitable for use.

History

Creation of Avtovo and launch of the newspaper page (1954)

In 1954, engineer Linohe proposed an infrastructure solution: to build a storage hub outside the city, which received the name “Avtovo”. A service town and a transport hub with an electric railway were planned nearby, while a closed zone contained multi-level parking facilities designed for long-term storage of large volumes of vehicles. The practice quickly proved effective: streets and courtyards were cleared without destroying the vehicle fleet.

The next problem was how to sell the accumulated cars. At the same time, the newspaper page “Auto-Nahodki” was launched: found vehicles received code numbers, their old registration plates from previous owners were removed, and temporary plates were installed for accounting and movement. Newspapers published new lots every day with short descriptions and inspection locations. The cars were sold cheaper than new ones, which made the market mass-accessible.

Private sales through the newspaper (1967)

In 1967, an initiative proposed by a local resident was approved: to use the popularity of Auto-Nahodki as the main listing channel and allow owners to sell their own cars through the same showcase. The format looked the same as with “found” lots: a photo, short description, and contacts. At the same time, the state retained 50% of the transaction value.

Despite the harsh conditions, the mechanism became popular: against the background of weak alternatives such as wall notices, marketplaces, and word of mouth, the newspaper provided fast reach and a clear procedure for the deal.

The 1972 reform: reduced deductions and paid promotion

In 1972, amid a softening of domestic policy and a change in public rhetoric, the publication rules were revised. The deduction from sales was reduced to 20% and was defined as payment for publication and distribution, essentially a commission or service fee. In addition, sellers gained the option to pay more for more frequent appearances of their listing in the issue, an early form of “promotion.”

The 1989 transition and development of the private format

After 1989, when the country left the communist model and declared a course toward a neutral technological power, the automotive market became sharply more complex: competition grew, new trade channels appeared, and demand for used cars changed. Even so, Auto-Nahodki did not disappear: the system was already familiar, and trust in the “single showcase” format remained high. Gradually, the project ceased to be “under the wing of the state” as an organizational unit and began operating as an independent structure, while preserving mechanisms for cooperation with the state on found vehicles.

Digital evolution

Text-based network catalogue (1992)

In 1992, Auto-Nahodki made its first digital step: a network catalogue appeared in text format with a DOS/terminal interface. The user could see current lots by connecting to the server: the list could be updated without waiting for the next newspaper issue. This launch is often called one of the early mass reasons to “go online” inside Kivish.

First HTML website (1995)

In 1995, the platform received a graphical web shell. The format was intentionally simple: a listing card with a photograph, a short description, a phone number, and the address of the seller or lot. New listings appeared when the page was refreshed, which at the time was perceived as “almost real time.”

CSS and early JavaScript (2001)

In 2001, the website received its first noticeable visual layer: early CSS styling elements appeared, the structure became cleaner, and later simple JavaScript scripts were added. The site began interacting with users more actively through forms, checks, expandable blocks, and the first convenient navigation elements, gradually moving away from the role of an “electronic newspaper” toward a full service.

Full abandonment of print (2010)

In 2010, after another update of the design and functionality, printed publication of new listings was discontinued: newspapers stopped placing car purchase and sale lots, and Auto-Nahodki fully moved into digital format. From that moment, the website became the single point for updating offers.

Operating scheme

Found vehicles

The modern flow of “found” cars is organized as a sequence of procedures:

  1. A vehicle is discovered on a street or problem parking area by state services, including the police.
  2. An investigation is carried out into the status of the vehicle and the circumstances of its appearance, including registration, inspection, and whether sale is appropriate.
  3. If a decision is made to sell it, the vehicle is transferred to the Auto-Nahodki platform.
  4. The platform publishes the lot on its own behalf: photographs, price, condition description, and inspection or re-registration terms.

Historically, waiting periods before final sale are applied to found vehicles: 2 years for ordinary vehicles and 4 years for expensive vehicles.

Private listings

Users and companies can publish listings themselves, selling and buying cars. Paid publication and promotion options remain within the service’s tradition, as the historical successor to the newspaper practice of “placing more often.”

Significance

  • Urban: rapid removal of abandoned cars from streets into controlled storage.
  • Social: mass availability of used cars through transparent circulation.
  • Market: formation of a stable secondary market and services around it, including inspections, repairs, and logistics.
  • Technological: one of the earliest and most widely used internet services in Kivish, active since 1992.

Criticism

Early forms of the project were criticized for the harshness of the UKR era and for the high deductions in the late 1960s, which reached 50%. After the 1972 reforms, criticism of the economic side became noticeably softer, and after 1989 the discussion shifted toward issues of competition and moderation rules on a single platform.
