AirHokol-66

AirHokol-66 is a Kivish state-owned aircraft manufacturing company founded in 1932. It is known for passenger aircraft with a high level of safety and distinctive emergency protection systems.

History

The company was established in 1932 as a producer of military aircraft. Its first aircraft was the single-seat AirHokol-66 010, used for army needs. For civil aviation in the 1930s, Kivish purchased aircraft in the USSR.

After World War II (1944), together with USSR engineers, the company released its first large passenger airliner, the AirHokol-66 M20, designed for 10 seats. From that moment, AirHokol-66 moved to its own mass developments.

Key aircraft models

  • M20 (1944) — the first passenger aircraft, 10 seats.
  • D100 (1963) — 124 seats, featuring the unique “clockwork stabilization system”.
  • Bi225 (1983) — the first wide-body airliner for 312 passengers, equipped with cameras and survival systems.
  • D124 (1999) — a mid-size airliner for 96 seats, with a reinforced fuselage.

Safety systems

AirHokol-66 is known for implementing distinctive solutions:

  • “Clockwork system” (1963) — an autonomous early anti-accident module for early AirHokol-66 aircraft (since 1963), built on clock principles: pendulum(s), escapement, cams/gear summators, spring barrels, and limited servos on cable control loops. Its task is early detection of dangerous bank/stall and gentle correction toward level flight, with parameter recording on a chart medium for the “black box”.
  • SSBS — (Ship Distress Triggering System for AirHokol-66) an automatic alert and telemetry transmission system for emergencies on aircraft where this system is installed.
  • QRC — (Quick Rescue Compartment) a unique structural element associated with the AirHokol-66 flight recorder system, first introduced in 1983 on the Bi225. Its task is to provide survivors with a minimal set of supplies for the first hours/days until rescuers arrive.
  • AIHP — artificial intelligence assistance for pilots (2020s).
  • Remote control via simulator — first used in 2038 during a hijacking incident.

International operations

The company supplies aircraft to multiple countries, including Russia, Japan, European countries, and Latin America. In the early 2000s, joint routes connected Kora, Moscow, Paris, and New York.

Stories that shocked the world

  • 1963 — Introduction of the “clockwork system”
    The world’s first mechanical anti-stall automatics based on pendulums and gears. The world heard for the first time that an airplane could itself save itself from a stall.
  • 1983 — Quick Rescue Compartment
    The Bi225 became the first airliner to include a compartment with blankets, water, and food. Newspapers wrote: “Now even after a crash, passengers have a chance to wait for rescuers.”
  • 2001 — Flight KA-D568 (D124) (September 11)
    An AirHokol-66 D124 struck a World Trade Center tower while keeping the fuselage largely intact. Thanks to fire suppression systems and the rescue compartment, dozens of people survived inside. Global media called it “the most unbelievable rescue of the 21st century.”
  • 2014 — Live broadcast of a JAL123 simulation
    A German blogger and engineers reconstructed the 1985 Japanese accident, but on a Bi225 airliner. Even without a vertical stabilizer, the aircraft managed to land in Tokyo. Millions watched the broadcast in Japan—and cried.

Major incidents

  • 2001 — flight KA-D568 (D124) survived the September 11 attacks, bringing the company worldwide attention.
  • 2006 — the G530 crash over the Black Sea; weather conditions were the cause.

Legacy

AirHokol-66 is considered one of the world leaders in aviation safety. Joint developments with Airbus after 2015 were implemented in most European airliners, and by the 2030s the company had established itself as a benchmark in crash prevention.
