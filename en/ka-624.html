Kivish Air Flight 624 crash

The KA-624 crash was the deadliest tragedy in the history of Kivish civil aviation. On May 25, 1994, a passenger Boeing 737-200 operating the route Kora — Lamya lost control and fell onto the busy KS-54 highway, striking a nearby railway line. Around 708 people were killed.

Aircraft and crew

Aircraft: Boeing 737-200. The airplane was purchased from the US side in early 1994 as “new”; later, the investigation established that the airframe had previously been operated and was refurbished before delivery.

  • Captain: Alante Madalkin, 67 — flight time: ~3,750 h on AirHokol-66 D115; ~2,600 h on Tu-154; ~1,700 h on Boeing 737.
  • First officer: Laya Marolgina, 26 — trainee.

Flight sequence

The flight began normally. During cruise, the first officer reported: “the yoke is not responding”; the autopilot was disengaged. Shortly after, the warning FLIGHT CONTROL PRIMARY SYSTEM appeared. The crew contacted ATC and switched to a backup frequency.

About 30 minutes later, pressure began dropping in both hydraulic systems (HYD A/B PRESSURE LOW), followed by failure (HYD A/B FAILURE) and the aircraft switching to direct control mode. According to the crew recordings, at that time the aircraft was over the Malsky Region (mountain terrain).

The captain attempted to hold the airplane with differential thrust; engine failures followed, and a fire broke out in the right engine. The cockpit continuously heard WHOOP WHOOP TERRAIN, PULL UP, STALL, OVERSPEED.

Crash

The aircraft impacted the multi-lane KS-54 highway during peak traffic, destroying many vehicles. A wing section fell onto the passing electric train DG-6648 (ER2P): three cars were destroyed, and the remaining cars derailed.

Damaged/destroyed road vehicles68 passenger cars; 32 trucks; 5 scheduled buses; 8 minibuses.
Rail consequencesER2P DG-6648 — 3 cars destroyed, the train derailed.
Fatalitiesaround 708 people.

Eyewitness account: the Fali family — a delay at a gas station (~12 minutes) helped them avoid the direct impact; they witnessed the crash.

Route Kora → Lamya; segment over mountain terrain (Malsky Region) and the impact point on KS-54.

Flight recorders

Cockpit voice and flight data recorders were decoded by an international team. Extracts include: “the yoke is not responding,” AUTOPILOT DISCONNECT, HYD A/B PRESSURE LOW, attempts at thrust control (“One! — Two!”), followed by ENGINE FAILURE and fire, and continuous ground-proximity and stall/overspeed warnings.

Investigation

The investigation was led by AirHokol-66 together with JTSB specialists under ICAO coordination; materials were forwarded to the UN and Interpol. It concluded a dual hydraulic failure and also established that the aircraft had previously been operated and refurbished before delivery while being sold as “new,” indicating an internal fraud scheme in the supply chain.

International reaction

  • Kivish: suspension of Boeing operations, large-scale fleet replacement with domestic and Russian aircraft.
  • Japan: support for the investigation and comparisons with earlier tragedies.
  • ICAO/UN/Interpol: audits of deliveries and certification processes were initiated.

Changes in aviation

  • A global flight-hours registry was created and aircraft-origin checks were tightened.
  • Requirements for control-system redundancy were strengthened.
  • Recommendations were introduced for routing over densely populated highways and transport hubs.

Memory

  • A memorial on the KS-54 segment; annual remembrance ceremonies on May 25.
  • Documentary film Hikot2008 “Kivish Air 624: The last flight”.
