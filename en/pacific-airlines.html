Pacific Airlines

Pacific Airlines is a private airline of Kivish, founded in 1992 in Shentahor. It is considered the country’s first private air carrier after the end of the communist period. The company became best known for the school and educational program “Board No. 15”, under which discounted transport was provided for children’s and educational groups.

The history of Pacific Airlines is usually divided into its early period using Tu-154MK aircraft, the formation of a special educational line, the crisis after the destruction of flight PFA-723 in 2003, the later renewal of the fleet, and the transition to a combination of regular passenger transport with a separate educational program.

History

Foundation and early period

Pacific Airlines was founded in 1992 in Shentahor. At the initial stage, the airline purchased three Tu-154MK aircraft from the Miatovsky Aviation Plant and opened its first international routes in the Pacific direction. In the early period, the carrier was regarded primarily as a symbol of the opening of the private civil aviation market in Kivish after 1989.

Formation of the “Board No. 15” program

In 1993, Pacific Airlines formalized a special program for transporting school and educational groups, which received the designation “Board No. 15”. Initially, it was used for flights to Kivish for excursions, educational programs, and visits to museums, space facilities, and technocratic centers. Later, the program was expanded and began to be used for educational flights between different countries.

Rejection of Boeing

In 1994, Pacific Airlines abandoned plans to purchase Boeing aircraft after the crash of Kivish Air Flight 624. After that, the carrier established a policy of operating aircraft from its own or regionally close production sphere.

Reputation as a specialized carrier

In the second half of the 1990s, Pacific Airlines became established as a carrier focused on educational and children’s groups. The company’s reputation was built on strengthened technical control, strict crew selection for “Board No. 15” flights, and limited commercial markups on educational transport. In 2000, some Tu-154 aircraft were modernized to meet environmental standards, and after the events of 11 September 2001, security procedures and cockpit protection were further tightened.

Flight PFA-723 and its consequences

In 2003, flight PFA-723, carrying South Korean schoolchildren returning from Kivish, was destroyed over the territory of North Korea. After the disaster, international operation of the Tu-154 was temporarily restricted, the children’s transport program was suspended, and some groups that remained abroad returned with the help of aircraft from the state airline Kivish Air.

In 2019, the flight recorders of the flight were discovered at former secret North Korean bases. Their decoding was completed in 2020 and confirmed that the aircraft had deviated from its route due to a systemic navigation error, after which it was hit by North Korean missiles. According to the recorders, the aircraft broke apart at an altitude of about 9 200 meters.

Technical renewal and international expansion

After the 2003 crisis, Pacific Airlines and MiAZ began a deep redesign of the Tu-154 navigation systems and cockpits. In the following years, aircraft with glass cockpits, GPS navigation, and a new instrument architecture entered the fleet. In 2005, educational transport stopped being limited to routes to Kivish and began to be used for children’s and educational trips between other countries.

In 2008, Yak-42D aircraft were introduced into the fleet for small groups, some of which later underwent additional modernization of the cockpit and safety systems. In 2013, Sukhoi Superjet 100 aircraft were purchased to replace some of these aircraft.

Transition to a new generation of fleet

In 2017, Pacific Airlines removed almost all Yak-42 aircraft from active operation, sold some Tu-154 aircraft, and purchased AirHokol-66 D134 aircraft. In 2018, the fleet was supplemented with two AirHokol-66 D140 aircraft, after which the company strengthened regular passenger transport and began considering more distant markets alongside the continued operation of the “Board No. 15” program.

Restrictions of the 2020s

Since 2022, Pacific Airlines has stopped regular private flights to the Russian Federation, while preserving the possibility of separate school and educational transport. Later, a similar formula was formally extended to the Ukrainian direction as well. In 2026, due to the growing threat from drones amid the expansion of the war, the company temporarily restricted or suspended some children’s flights to Russia, Ukraine, and a number of neighboring Eastern European countries; a softer regime after negotiations was preserved for certain destinations, including Belarus, Poland, and Finland.

Operations

In different periods, Pacific Airlines combined two main operating models: regular passenger transport and the special educational program “Board No. 15”. Since the second half of the 2000s, educational flights have been operated not only to Kivish, but also between other countries under a discounted scheme for schools, museums, educational centers, and children’s exchange programs.

Fleet

  • Tu-154MK — main aircraft type. Currently 10 aircraft.
  • Yak-42D — small aircraft type purchased as crisis-survival aircraft during the aviation fuel crisis. Currently 2 aircraft in reserve in the hangars of Shentahor Airport.
  • Sukhoi Superjet 100 — medium aircraft type purchased to replace older aircraft used on “Board No. 15” flights. Currently 4 aircraft.
  • AirHokol-66 D134 — main medium-haul aircraft type for regular flights. Currently 4 aircraft.
  • AirHokol-66 D140 — larger aircraft that strengthened the fleet. Currently 2 aircraft.
