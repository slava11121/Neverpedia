Veneligi

Veneligi is a Kivish helicopter manufacturer founded on 20 August 1955 by engineer Firog Vanelig. The company is considered one of the basic helicopter schools of the UKR and Kivish: from the first army machines of the 1950s to civilian, police, medical, and firefighting series, as well as closed military projects.

History

Creation of the bureau (1953–1955)

In 1953, engineer Firog Vanelig, having noticed the development of Soviet helicopters, proposed to Karas Shentakos that the UKR begin its own production of rotorcraft. Karas refused, believing that purchasing machines from the USSR would be simpler and more rational.

Vanelig’s idea was revisited in 1955. Against the background of growing distrust inside the communist bloc, Karas began looking for ways to increase the technological independence of the UKR. On 20 August 1955, the Vanelig Design Bureau was created, later establishing the name Veneligi.

First developments (1956–1959)

On 22 September 1956, the company’s first helicopter was assembled at the plant in Kora — the VL H10, an early-generation army machine. In 1957, the bureau began developing its own engine manufacturing for helicopters.

In 1958, the VG H30-2 was presented — a high-speed tandem-rotor transport helicopter, partially based on solutions from heavy transport machines of that period. It was created as a means for rapidly moving cargo and people.

In 1959, Veneligi released its first specialized machines for internal services:

  • VG P-40 — a four-seat police helicopter;
  • VG HH-5 — a medical helicopter with built-in stretchers;
  • VG FH-369 — a firefighting helicopter based on solutions from the H30-2 family.

Military radicalization of the Karas period (1960–1968)

In the early 1960s, Veneligi was increasingly drawn into closed army programs. According to documents later disclosed, some projects of this period were created under severe political pressure, and the testing of some combat solutions was connected with repressive practices in the Korean direction.

In 1965, the bureau completed one of the best-known combat projects of the Karas era — the VG AFK-6789, a high-speed machine-gun helicopter. In 1966, Firog Vanelig attempted to distance himself from what was happening and left several messages in which he wrote that he had not wanted to turn his work into a tool of killing. Some of these letters later surfaced outside the country.

In 1967, during a short pause, the bureau still managed to update its civilian line and release the VG P-60, VG HH-9, and VG FH-540. However, already in 1968, Firog Vanelig died under circumstances that were later recognized as a political murder. After his death, the company came under state control.

Decline of the late Karas period (1968–1972)

After nationalization, the enterprise began operating less and less stably. Karas tried to soften public discontent by releasing “kind” civilian helicopters for the population and services, but the quality of these machines turned out to be noticeably lower than that of the equipment from Vanelig’s own period. The plant worked in haste, production moved in fits and starts, and some initiatives remained at the level of unfinished series.

By 1971, the general crisis of the UKR had also affected the bureau: production effectively declined, and the enterprise was among the structures that the late regime no longer controlled systematically. After Karas’s death in 1972, a new stage in the company’s history began.

Mirim’s reforms and the return of the Vanelig school (1972–1980)

After Mirim came to power, the previous line was publicly revised. The company received the opportunity to return to normal development, and the heir of the murdered designer became his son, Ino Vanelig. One of the first steps of the new period was the restoration of the bureau’s reputation and the rejection of the practices of the Karas era.

Under Ino Vanelig’s leadership, work began on new firefighting, transport, and special helicopters. After the release of the VG FH-555, the old plant was closed and production was moved to the city of Malta. The new plant was built on a separately purchased plot of land. At the entrance, a monument to the bureau’s first helicopter and a memorial to Firog Vanelig called “Killed for the Truth” were installed.

In 1978, the company released the light civilian helicopter VeLi 100. The machine was created as a small two-seat helicopter for local residents and the training segment. It was based on solutions from the class of light American machines, but the design was reworked for local conditions and safety standards.

In 1980, the company updated its service line:

  • VeLi P50 — a new-generation police helicopter;
  • VeLi H80 — a completely redesigned medical helicopter;
  • VeLi F70 — a new firefighting machine with increased water capacity.

Late UKR period and entry into the external market (1980s — early 1990s)

In the 1980s, the company mainly developed its existing police, firefighting, medical, and civilian series. At the same time, closed army orders for new models were carried out, but they were almost never disclosed.

After the country left the communist bloc in 1989, Veneligi began working more actively in the civilian market. The company immediately expanded its design programs for the public sector and began sales to the West. One export model was the VeLi 190, which was perceived as an alternative to American civilian helicopters.

DFTR-2479 project (1992)

In 1992, the company unexpectedly returned to the experimental combat theme and tested the high-speed and highly maneuverable four-seat helicopter VeLi DFTR-2479. The machine was conceived as a successor to the combat projects of the Karas period, but already without their political and repressive context.

The tests were shown only in photo and video materials, after which the helicopter was moved into a closed army circuit. In aviation circles, the DFTR-2479 received the nickname “Dragonfly”. Despite its limited publicity, this machine was long considered the fastest and most maneuverable development of the Veneligi school.

Civilian stabilization and the rare return of “Dragonfly” (1990s — 2020s)

After the 1990s, the company stopped making sharp leaps and developed as an ordinary helicopter manufacturer. Its core activity became civilian, police, firefighting, and medical machines, as well as deliveries to the external market. In the West, Veneligi gradually established itself as one of the manufacturers included in the purchasing lists of civilian operators alongside other popular brands.

The company experimented with safety solutions, but some ideas did not reach production because of high cost or weak practical benefit. During this period, the DFTR-2479 remained a closed development and was almost never mentioned publicly.

During the African campaign, which ended in 2021, the Kivish army used the VeLi DFTR-2479 on a limited basis. The machines were used as a rare force trump card at the final stage of the campaign. After the end of combat operations, the “Dragonflies” were again withdrawn into the closed army circuit and returned to bases.

After that, Veneligi finally returned to the role of an ordinary helicopter manufacturer, remaining at the same level as other well-known companies in the industry.

Key models

  • VL H10 (1956) — the company’s first army helicopter.
  • VG H30-2 (1958) — a high-speed tandem-rotor transport helicopter.
  • VG P-40 (1959) — a police helicopter.
  • VG HH-5 (1959) — a medical helicopter.
  • VG FH-369 (1959) — a firefighting helicopter.
  • VG AFK-6789 (1965) — a combat helicopter of the Karas period.
  • VG P-60 (1967) — an updated police helicopter.
  • VG HH-9 (1967) — an updated medical helicopter.
  • VG FH-540 (1967) — an updated firefighting helicopter.
  • VG FH-555 — a transition-period model before the plant relocation.
  • VeLi 100 (1978) — a light two-seat civilian helicopter.
  • VeLi P50 (1980) — a new-generation police helicopter.
  • VeLi H80 (1980) — a new-generation medical helicopter.
  • VeLi F70 (1980) — a new-generation firefighting helicopter.
  • VeLi 190 — a civilian export model.
  • VeLi DFTR-2479 (1992) — a closed army development.

International activity

After 1989, the company began selling civilian models outside Kivish. In the external market, Veneligi was viewed as one of the alternative manufacturers of light and service helicopters, competing with American and European machines in individual segments.

Legacy

Veneligi occupies a noticeable place in the history of Kivish helicopter manufacturing. The company went from an early army bureau of the UKR era to a major manufacturer of civilian and service helicopters. A separate place in its history is held by the figure of Firog Vanelig and the later restoration of his name during the leadership of Ino Vanelig.
