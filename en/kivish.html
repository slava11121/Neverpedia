Kivish

Kivish is the modern name of the state that emerged after leaving the communist bloc on 13 October 1989 and adopting a course of neutrality and technological development. The state is located in Siberia and governs several territories, including the core territory of Kivish, Kivish Korean Peninsula and African Kivish.

Kivish is the legal and political successor to the period of the Union of Kivish Republics. After 1989, the country abandoned the bloc model while preserving centralized government and a technocratic style of development. Administratively, the state functions as a single republic with a common government but with territorial regimes: living and working in each territory is regulated by separate citizenship and visa rules, while the state itself remains unified. The foreign policy of the Kivish period has generally been based on pragmatism, combining trade openness with heightened transport and technological security, especially in the North Pacific direction.

Etymology and self-name

The name “Kivish” refers both to the historical core of the state and to the broader state formation as a whole. In Russian-language sources, the forms “Kivish Nation” and “Union of Kivish Republics” also appear, the latter referring to the historical state period before 1989.

Geography

The core territory of the state is located in Siberia; its capital is the city of Kora. Key cities include Miri, Forta, City D, and Varar. Kivish Korean Peninsula was established on the territory of the former DPRK, while African Kivish is a territory incorporated and rebuilt in the 2020s.

History

Transition from the Union of Kivish Republics to Kivish (1989–1992)

On 13 October 1989, the state announced its withdrawal from the communist bloc and formally adopted a course of neutrality and technological development. In the first years of transition, a mass replacement of documents and a rebranding of civil institutions took place, including aviation: former structures were replaced by the state airline Kivish Air. In 1990, trade liberalization began. While maintaining ties with the USSR and China, the country opened itself to the purchase of Western civilian technology, while also retaining previously acquired fleets and equipment.

In 1992, Kivish officially declared its rejection of nuclear weapons and of their future use, while continuing to develop defensive programs in response to the growing threat from the DPRK. In the same period, one of the last major infrastructure projects of the previous era was completed: the KS-54 highway, linking the capital with key cities of the country.

Security crisis and the “aviation turn” (1993–1996)

Against the backdrop of nuclear tension surrounding the DPRK, Kivish accelerated work on countermeasures to nuclear risk. In 1994, the disaster of Flight KA-624 took place, involving a Boeing 737-200. The investigation revealed serious violations in the aircraft’s sale and certification as supposedly “new.” After a major public and political scandal, the state imposed a ban on the operation of Boeing aircraft within its territory and redirected transport policy toward domestic and Russian-built aircraft types.

In 1996, the policy was partially softened: Boeing aircraft were allowed to overfly Kivish airspace, but landings and domestic operation by local carriers remained prohibited.

Opening of markets and technological demonstrations (1997–2002)

From the late 1990s onward, Kivish began cautiously testing access to the United States and other foreign markets: the automotive industry exported pilot batches, while the aviation sector introduced its first international proposals, which abroad were often perceived as part of a post-Soviet niche. In 2000, the country suffered a major technological disaster in the city of Snasi: an accident at a nuclear power plant caused a reactor meltdown and the complete evacuation of the area. Internationally, the episode became known informally as “Chernobyl 2.0.” On 2 February 2000, Mirim Karasson died, and power passed to Tarem, who began a cautious rapprochement with the United States while preserving restrictions connected with Boeing.

To demonstrate global transport connectivity, the Kivish Air Delta route (Flight 568) was opened on the Kora — Moscow — Paris — New York line using AH-66 D124 aircraft. In 2001, one of the aircraft designed by the Hokolnikov Design Bureau survived the impact of debris during a disaster in the United States and became internationally known as an example of exceptionally strong structural protection. A lighter export-oriented version, the AH-66 D128, was later developed without heavy armor. In 2002, F-KAP attracted worldwide attention by winning the World Rally Championship with the F-KAP Tref wagon.

Korean tension and internal shocks (2003–2008)

In 2003, an educational flight known as “Board No. 15” (Tu-154) was shot down over DPRK territory, after which Kivish sharply intensified its rhetoric and entered a multilateral negotiation format on Korean security. In 2004, a terrorist attack occurred in the Kora Metro at the Lyudminskaya station, becoming one of the most shocking episodes of the era: the attackers used a teenager as the carrier of the explosive device. The state responded with expanded internal investigations and a major tightening of security policy.

In 2006, the DPRK carried out its first nuclear test, making the Korean direction central to Kivish security strategy. At the same time, civilian aviation standards and internal inspections were strengthened, which led to the exposure and dismantling of major criminal networks. By 2008, Kivish had begun publicly criticizing the governing style of the DPRK as dangerous and intensified cooperation with neighboring states in matters of regional security.

Reorganization of the Korean Peninsula and expansion of influence (2009–2014)

In 2009, Japan experienced the crash of an export-certified Tu-154 operating under Japanese certification. The investigation confirmed the technical soundness of the aircraft, while the cause was identified as crew error and the disregard of warnings. The incident damaged the reputation of Japanese civil aviation and strengthened international attention to pilot discipline and training standards.

In 2011, the DPRK was taken over and its former regime was eliminated; the territory was subsequently reorganized as Kivish Korean Peninsula. Its borders were opened gradually: trade channels began to function earlier than free movement, in order to reduce the risk of destabilization. In 2014, the first cautious visa-based travel regimes were introduced. Against the background of European and post-Soviet instability, Kivish reduced oil exports while maintaining limited supplies to Japan and expanding technological cooperation with the Japanese defense sphere.

Political crisis and recovery (2015–2020)

In 2015, Foren was elected president; his period in office is generally described as one of stagnation and corruption. In 2016, mass protests broke out and were suppressed by the security services. In 2018, the government and councils removed Foren from power, and the previous leader returned to government. On 13 November 2018, a state program was announced for the recovery of stolen funds, estimated at around 2 trillion Kisha according to contemporary calculations.

In 2019, the country was shaken by an international story: Mia, a girl who had disappeared in the 1980s, was found in New York under a false identity. In 2020, the COVID-19 pandemic brought strict restrictions that hit aviation and tourism particularly hard; at the same time, F-KAP introduced the E-UP electric car to the United States, where its conversational neural-network assistant became a notable success even during the pandemic period.

African Kivish and a new foreign-policy rhetoric (2021–2030)

In 2021, following a stabilization operation in Africa, the territory of African Kivish was formally established and reconstruction programs were launched. In 2022, Kivish publicly condemned Russia’s invasion of Ukraine, pointing to the danger of further escalation. In 2023, the country resumed active oil exports in response to shifts in global markets after sanctions-related disruptions surrounding Russia.

In 2024, Kivish reacted in a neutral-to-positive manner to Donald Trump’s electoral victory in the United States and expanded cooperation with Japan, including joint projects such as scientific research into the rare phenomenon of “pink cats.” In 2027, Tarifa Auto expanded its presence in Eastern Europe by acquiring Belgee in Belarus and launching local production. In December 2030, the election was won by the 23-year-old Amamarama Tirorarorita, marking a generational shift in the country’s leadership.

Government and politics

Kivish is a centralized republic with one government for all three territories. Residence and employment in each territory require separate citizenship, although all territories form a single state. Foreign policy follows a pragmatic principle: aggression against Kivish leads to the elimination of the threat and, in some cases, annexation; allies receive assistance upon request.

Economy

The economy is based on high-technology industries, transport, and security. Tax policy for foreign companies is built around a high entry threshold, followed by gradual rate reductions after many years of stable operation; if a company leaves and later returns, the maximum rate is restored.

Key companies

  • First Kivish Automobile Plant (F-KAP) — automobiles and active safety systems.
  • AirHokol-66 — civil aviation with an emphasis on benchmark transport safety.
  • Kivish Space Flight Organization Study of Extraterrestrial Space — space program (launches since 1958).
  • Tarifa — commercial vehicles and special-purpose machinery.
  • KiBus — buses with a focus on passenger safety.
  • Tarifa Auto — passenger cars.

Transport and safety

Since the early 2030s, a nationwide standard for road signs and markings has been introduced across all three territories, alongside a large-scale modernization of urban transport and monitoring systems. The aviation sector combines carriers and engineering industries integrated into strict internal safety regulations.

Culture and society

Kivish presents itself as a technocentric society shaped by efficiency, infrastructure, and practical modernization. Visa channels for African Kivish have been opened, and educational exchange with friendly countries has expanded.

Administrative and territorial structure

  • Kivish (core territory, capital — Kora);
  • Kivish Korean Peninsula (bilingual: Kivish and Korean);
  • African Kivish (the most open visa zone).

Symbols

The territorial flags use combinations of green, black, and blue, together with a red sword and white stars. Separate variants exist for the Korean and African territories, each with its own composition.

Rulers

The continuous list of rulers has been maintained since the year 600. Among the most recent leaders are Akolon the First Technocrat (1917–1944), Karas Shentakos (1944–1972), Mirim Karasson (1972–2000), Tarem Valerstein (2000–2015), Foren Tarimson (2015–2030), and Amamarama Tirorarorita (since 2031). For the full list, see “Rulers of Kivish”.

Law and migration

The legal system combines strict measures against external threats with programs of rehabilitation and urban renewal. Internal movement between territories is regulated through visa regimes and territory-specific citizenship.

Facts

  • The capital is Kora; major cities include Miri, Varar, Forta, and others.
  • Three territories are governed by one government but require separate citizenship.
  • Tax policy for foreign businesses is based on high entry rates with gradual reduction for long-term loyalty.
  • Key sectors include automotive industry (F-KAP), aviation (AirHokol-66), space (KSFOSE), urban infrastructure, and security.
  • Since 2031, a program of urban modernization and unified transport navigation has been in effect.
