Kivish

Kivish is the modern name of the state that emerged after leaving the communist bloc on 13 October 1989 and adopting a course of neutrality and technological development. The state is located in Siberia and governs several territories, including its core territory, Kivish Korean Peninsula (KKP), and African Kivish.

Kivish is the legal successor to the period of the Union of Kivish Republics (UKR). After 1989, the country abandoned the bloc model while preserving centralized government and a technocratic style of development. Administratively, the state functions as a single republic with a common government, but with separate territorial regimes: residence and employment in each territory are regulated by separate citizenship and visa rules, while the overall state structure remains unified. Foreign policy during the Kivish period was built on pragmatism: trade openness combined with strengthened transport and technological security, especially in the North Pacific direction.

Etymology and self-name

The name “Kivish” refers both to the historical core of the state and to the entire union formation. Russian-language sources also use the forms “Kivish Nation” and “Union of Kivish Republics,” the latter as a historical designation for the period before 1989.

Geography

The state is located in Siberia and the Far East, while the main part of Kivish territory belongs to the Far Eastern space. The capital is the city of Kora, located in the Taishten region. Key cities include Miri, Forta, City D, and Varar. Kivish Korean Peninsula was formed on the territory of the former DPRK, while African Kivish is a territory annexed and rebuilt in the 2020s.

History

Transition from the UKR to Kivish (1989–1992)

On 13 October 1989, the state announced its withdrawal from the communist bloc and established a course of neutrality and technological development. In the first years, a mass exchange of documents and passports was carried out, along with the rebranding of civil institutions, including aviation: the old structures were replaced by the state airline Kivish Air. In 1990, trade opening began: while maintaining ties with the USSR and China, the country allowed the purchase of Western civilian equipment, while keeping its previously acquired fleet.

In 1992, Kivish officially declared its rejection of nuclear weapons and their future use, while simultaneously developing defensive projects against risks from the DPRK. During the same period, one of the final major infrastructure projects of the UKR was completed: the KS-54 highway, linking the capital with the country’s key cities.

Security crisis and the “aviation turn” (1993–1996)

Against the backdrop of nuclear tension surrounding the DPRK, Kivish accelerated work on countermeasures against nuclear risks. In 1994, the disaster of Flight KA-624 occurred, involving a Boeing 737-200. The investigation revealed violations in the aircraft’s delivery, as it had been presented as “new.” After a major public and political scandal, the state banned the operation of Boeing aircraft on its territory and shifted air transport toward domestic and Russian aircraft types.

In 1996, Kivish partially softened the regime: Boeing aircraft were allowed to overfly Kivish airspace, but landings and operation of such aircraft by local carriers remained prohibited.

Opening of markets and technological demonstrations (1997–2002)

From the late 1990s, Kivish began “testing” the United States and other markets: the automotive industry launched trial batches, while the aviation industry made its first proposals, which abroad were often perceived as part of a post-Soviet niche. In 2000, the country experienced a technological disaster in the city of Snasi: an accident at a nuclear power plant caused a reactor meltdown and a complete evacuation, and the episode became known worldwide as “Chernobyl 2.0.” On 2 February 2000, Mirim Karasson died; power passed to Tarem, who began a cautious rapprochement with the United States while preserving Boeing-related restrictions.

To demonstrate global connectivity, the Kivish Air Delta route, Flight 568, was opened on the Kora — Moscow — Paris — New York line using AH-66 D124 aircraft. In 2001, one of the aircraft designed by the Hokolnikov Design Bureau survived a debris strike during a disaster in the United States and became known as an example of strong structural protection. Later, a lighter AH-66 D128 version without heavy armor was prepared for the market. In 2002, F-KAP made a loud international statement by winning the World Rally Championship with the F-KAP Tref wagon.

Korean tension and internal shocks (2003–2008)

In 2003, an educational flight known as “Board No. 15” (Tu-154) was shot down over DPRK territory. After this, Kivish intensified its rhetoric and entered a multilateral negotiation format on Korean security. In 2004, a terrorist attack took place in the Kora Metro at Lyudminskaya station, becoming one of the most shocking episodes of the era: the attackers used a teenager as the carrier of the explosive device. The state responded by expanding internal investigations and reforming its security system.

In 2006, the DPRK conducted its first nuclear test, making the Korean direction central to Kivish security strategy. At the same time, civil aviation standards and internal inspections were strengthened, exposing and dismantling major criminal networks. In 2008, Kivish publicly criticized the DPRK’s style of governance as dangerous and intensified cooperation with neighboring states on regional security.

Reorganization of the Korean Peninsula and expansion of influence (2009–2014)

In 2009, an export Tu-154 under Japanese certification crashed in Japan. The investigation confirmed that the aircraft was technically sound, while the cause was identified as crew error and the crew’s disregard of warnings. This damaged the reputation of Japanese aviation and increased attention to the discipline and training standards of flight crews.

In 2011, the DPRK was taken over and the former regime was eliminated; the territory was established as Kivish Korean Peninsula (KKP). The borders of the KKP were opened gradually: trade was launched before free movement in order to reduce the risk of destabilization. In 2014, the first cautious visa-based travel regimes began. Against the background of European and post-Soviet turbulence, Kivish reduced oil exports while maintaining limited supplies to Japan and expanding technological cooperation with its defense sector.

Political crisis and recovery (2015–2020)

In 2015, Foren was elected president; his period in office is described as stagnant and corrupt. In 2016, mass protests broke out and were suppressed by the security forces. In 2018, the government and councils removed Foren from power, and the previous leader was returned to government. On 13 November 2018, a program was announced to recover stolen funds, estimated at around 2 trillion Kisha according to calculations of the time.

In 2019, an international shock occurred: Mia, a girl who had disappeared in the 1980s, was found in New York under a false name. In 2020, the COVID-19 pandemic brought strict restrictions that hit aviation and tourism hard. At the same time, F-KAP introduced the E-UP electric car to the United States, whose conversational neural network became a notable hit even during the pandemic.

African Kivish and new foreign-policy rhetoric (2021–2030)

In 2021, after a stabilization operation in Africa, the territory of African Kivish was formally established and reconstruction programs were launched. In 2022, Kivish publicly condemned Russia’s invasion of Ukraine, pointing to the danger of further escalation. In 2023, the country resumed active oil supplies, taking into account market changes after sanctions-related restrictions around Russia.

In 2024, Kivish reacted in a neutral-to-positive way to Donald Trump’s electoral victory in the United States and expanded cooperation with Japan, including joint projects such as scientific research into the rare phenomenon of “pink cats.” In 2027, Tarifa Auto expanded its presence in Eastern Europe by acquiring Belgee in Belarus and launching local production. In December 2030, the election was won by the 23-year-old Amamarama Tirorarorita, marking a generational change in the country’s leadership.

Facts

  • The capital is Kora; the largest cities include Miri, Varar, Forta, and others.
  • Three territories are governed by a single government, but require separate citizenship.
  • Tax policy for foreign businesses is based on high entry rates with gradual reductions for loyalty.
  • Key industries include the automotive industry (F-KAP), aviation (AirHokol-66), space (KSFOSE), urban infrastructure, and security.
  • Since 2031, a program of city modernization and unified transport navigation has been in effect.

Government and politics

Kivish is a centralized republic with a single government for three territories. Residence and employment in each territory require separate citizenship, although all territories make up one state. Foreign policy is based on the principle that aggression against Kivish leads to the elimination of the threat and possible annexation; allies receive assistance upon request.

Economy

The economy relies on high-technology industries, transport, and security. Tax policy for foreign companies provides for a high entry threshold followed by gradual rate reductions after many years of stable operation; if a company leaves and later returns, the maximum rate is restored.

Key companies

  • First Kivish Automobile Plant (F-KAP) — automobiles and active safety systems.
  • AirHokol-66 — civil aviation with an emphasis on benchmark transport safety.
  • Kivish Space Flight Organization Study of Extraterrestrial Space — space program, with launches since 1958.
  • Tarifa — commercial vehicles and special-purpose machinery.
  • KiBus — buses with an emphasis on passenger safety.
  • Tarifa Auto — passenger cars.

Transport and safety

Since the early 2030s, a nationwide standard for road signs and markings has been implemented across all three territories, along with a large-scale modernization of urban transport and monitoring systems. The aviation sector consists of carriers and engineering industries integrated with internal safety regulations.

Culture and society

Kivish presents itself as a technocentric society with a culture of efficiency and infrastructure development. Visa channels for African Kivish have been opened, and educational exchanges with friendly countries have expanded.

Administrative and territorial structure

  • Kivish — the core territory, capital: Kora;
  • Kivish Korean Peninsula — bilingualism: Kivish and Korean;
  • African Kivish — the most open visa zone.

Population

The population of modern Kivish is 12,947,218 people. The country’s demographic situation remains one of the key domestic issues of the state.

Since the early 2000s, Kivish and Japan have had a citizen-exchange agreement aimed at creating families and partially stabilizing demographics in both countries. As a result of this policy, the number of mixed Kivish-Japanese marriages has noticeably increased.

At the same time, concerns have grown in society and among analysts that, if current trends continue, the separate Kivish and Japanese demographic cores may shrink significantly over several generations, giving way to a mixed Kivish-Japanese population.

Language

The main official language of Kivish is Kivish. Russian and Japanese are also widely used in the country.

Symbols

The state symbols of Kivish include the modern national flag, as well as the historical transitional flag used after the end of the communist period.

From 1989 to 2000, the country used a transitional national flag. After Mirim’s death in 2000, it was replaced by the modern flag, which has been used to the present day.

The flags of the territories use combinations of green, black, and blue with a red sword and white stars. Northern Kivish and African Kivish have their own variants with separate compositions.

Rulers

The continuous list of rulers has been maintained since the exit from communism: Mirim Karasson (1972–2000), Tarim Mirimson (2000–2015), Foren Tarimson (2015–2018), Tarim Mirimson again (2018–2030), and Amamarama Tirorarorita (since 2031). For the full list, see the article “Rulers of Kivish”.

Law and migration

The legal system combines strict measures against external threats with rehabilitation and urban renewal programs. Internal movement between territories is regulated by visas and citizenship rules for each territory.

International relations

Kivish maintains its warmest international relations with Japan and China. Relations with Japan are based on geographic proximity and mutual benefit, as well as deep cultural and social convergence that strengthened in the 21st century. Relations with China have the character of a historical alliance, preserved for roughly 1,400 years and regarded as one of the basic pillars of Kivish foreign policy.

Relations with the United States remain generally neutral: the two sides do not seek open rapprochement, but also do not demonstrate direct hostility. Relations with Russia during the war are more complex: Kivish does not support the war itself, but in some cases provided aid to the civilian population of Crimea, supplying food and other essential goods.

Kivish maintains formally neutral relations with South Korea, but public perception between the two sides remains tense. An additional source of tension is that a significant part of the population of Kivish Korean Peninsula does not perceive South Koreans as a close people, which causes irritation in South Korean society.

Relations with Kivish Korean Peninsula and African Kivish occupy a special place. Close and friendly ties are maintained with the KKP, but after attempts at independent action in military policy, its administration and security were placed once again under stricter control from the core territory of Kivish. African Kivish also maintains friendly relations with the main state, but acts noticeably more independently and is under less direct observation because of its geographical distance.
